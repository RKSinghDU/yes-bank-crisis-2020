How the Information Cascade Unfolded

Stage 1: Private Uncertainty (February 2020)

Each depositor evaluates bank safety independently. Most lack expertise to assess balance sheet. Private signal: "Probably okay, but I'm not 100% sure." Uncertainty is high.

Stage 2: Observing Others (Late February)

See ATM queues, news reports of deposit outflows. Social proof kicks in: "Others are withdrawing. Why? Do they know something?" Observation becomes more important than private assessment.

Stage 3: Rational Calculation (Early March)

If I withdraw and bank is fine: I lose some interest (small cost).
If I don't withdraw and bank fails: I lose everything (huge cost).
Dominant strategy: WITHDRAW. This is individually rational.

Stage 4: The Cascade (March 5-6)

Each withdrawal reinforces others' decisions. "Everyone is withdrawing‚Äîthey must know something." Original private information completely ignored. Cascade becomes self-fulfilling. Collectively irrational outcome emerges from individually rational decisions.

Behavioral Economics Lessons

1. Rational Individuals ‚â† Rational Outcomes

Every YES Bank depositor acted rationally given uncertainty and observable actions of others. No one made a "mistake." Yet collectively, they created an unnecessary crisis. This is the paradox of information cascades: individually optimal decisions lead to socially suboptimal outcomes.

2. Observability Accelerates Modern Cascades

In 1991 (Harshad Mehta scam), bank runs took weeks to develop via newspapers and TV. In 2020 (YES Bank), the cascade happened in 48 hours via real-time social media, WhatsApp forwards, and live TV coverage of bank queues. Digital age dramatically accelerates financial panics.

3. Government Backing Is Necessary But Not Sufficient

RBI guaranteed all deposits. Didn't prevent panic. Why? Uncertainty about WHEN depositors would get money back. Liquidity needs are immediate (payroll, bills, EMIs). "Eventually safe" doesn't help with next week's obligations. Behavioral reality trumps economic theory.

4. Intervention Timing Creates Coordination Problems

The moratorium (meant to stabilize the bank) was interpreted as confirmation of danger, triggering the very panic it aimed to prevent. Game theory: Multiple equilibria exist. Bad equilibrium (panic) is stable once started. Moving to good equilibrium requires credible external force.

5. "Too Big To Fail" Creates Moral Hazard

Depositors believed YES Bank (4th largest) would be rescued, enabling risky lending without depositor discipline. Reality: Government DID rescue it, but with pain (restrictions, dilution). Validates TBTF but shows it's not painless. System remains fragile.

Case Study Summary: The YES Bank crisis demonstrates how information cascades work in modern financial systems. Rational individual behavior can create irrational collective outcomes when people observe and react to each other rather than to fundamentals.
